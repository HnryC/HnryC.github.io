Alpha Decay

  • Only occurs in nuclei with an atomic number greater than 100
  • Nuclei releases an alpha particle
  • Atomic number decreases by 2 and atomic mass decreases by 4
  • The alpha particle has the lowest penetrating power of radiation, and it can be stopped with a thick layer of skin or paper
  • The alpha particle has the highest ionising power of radiation
  • Occurs in uranium-238 decay into thorium-234

Beta Decay

  • Occurs in nuclei with too many proton or neutrons.
  • Nuclei emits an beta particle and either converts a neutron to a proton or converts a proton to a neutron.
  • Releases a positive beta particle if a proton gets converted to a neutron
  • Releases a negative beta particle if a neutron gets converted to a proton
  • Atomic number increases by 1 and atomic mass stays the same
  • The beta particle's penetrating power is less than gamma radiation, but greater than that of an alpha particle, aluminium to stop it
  • The ionising power of the beta particle resides between that of an alpha particle and a gamma ray
  • Occurs in carbon-14 decay into nitrogen-14

Gamma Decay

  • Occurs when a high energy nuclei looses energy
  • Nuclei emits a gamma ray when an high energy nuclei looses energy
  • Atomic number and atomic mass stay the same

Half Life

  • The average time it takes for half the particles in a sample to undergo neucular decay
